# A Love Letter to Ignition IT

I didn't plan any of this.

I was studying forensic anthropology at UMass Boston. I was always more interested in the bones than the crime scene part. The crime scene part was just how you got to handle bones without doing a long stretch in federal prison. That's a joke. Mostly.

I did a lot of independent study with my mentor in the anthropology department. One week he handed me a skull and told me to come back with at least a hundred things to say about it. I took notes, I did the work, and I drove around for a week with a human skull in my trunk. We met the following week and went through my findings. On my way out the door he mentioned, almost as an aside, that I should probably know those were remains from a Native tribe, and that if I had crossed state lines with them at any point during the week, I would have violated federal law governing the transport of human remains.

He waited a week to tell me that.

Somewhere between the skulls and the federal statutes, I took a student worker job at the information systems help desk to pay the bills. I have never taken a computer science course in my life. What I found was that I was good at it, I liked it, and in that help desk office I discovered the Mac. That was it. Career decided. Bones could wait.

That was 1992. I have been doing this ever since.

My career has a pattern I've only recently been able to name: I show up after things have gone sideways. Not disasters exactly, but places where the foundation was never quite right, or the last person built something that worked until it didn't, or nobody ever had the time to do it properly. I walk in, figure out what's actually broken versus what just looks broken, and I build. Sometimes from scratch. Sometimes on top of rubble. Either way, by the time I leave, the thing works. More importantly, the people using it can trust it.

I've been a Mac admin since the Classic OS days. I've been a Jamf admin since Jamf was still called Casper and lived in Minnesota. I've built one-to-one laptop programs for schools, zero-touch deployment pipelines for startups, compliance baselines for security teams, and identity workflows that get a new Mac from box to fully-configured in under twenty minutes without a human touching it. I know this stuff at a depth that comes from doing it wrong enough times to know how to do it right.

That said: I support Windows too. I have since XP. I don't love it the way I love macOS, but that's not the point. My driving philosophy. The one thing I'll push back on anyone about: is that end users should use the tool that suits them best, and it's my job to make that tool work. Doesn't matter what it is.

Outside of work I am what my people call an alpha geek. I paint Warhammer 40K miniatures. I read science fiction and fantasy. I've been playing the same World of Warcraft character for seventeen years. I quote Dune at inappropriate moments. My humor is bone dry. I am far more likely to drop a perfectly timed one-liner than to tell you a joke, but if you insist on a joke:

*How do elephants hide in trees? They paint their balls yellow.*

*How was Tarzan killed? Picking grapefruit.*

My dad told me those. I've told my son. The cycle continues.

I used Claude (Anthropic's AI) to help me shape and write this letter. I gave it the raw material (the real answers, the actual stories, the terrible jokes) and it helped me turn that into something readable. The thoughts are mine. The structure is collaborative. I figure if you're going to ask about AI use, you deserve a straight answer.

Why Ignition? Because what you describe: parachuting into companies and helping them build the infrastructure they need. That sounds like what I've been doing my whole career, just without the variety. The MSP model plays to the thing I'm actually good at. I'm not looking for a job where I maintain something stable. I want to build things. I want problems that are actually problems. I want to be useful.

I think I'd fit your "odd batter."

Eric Young
